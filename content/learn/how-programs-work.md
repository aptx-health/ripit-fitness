# How Programs Work

A training program is a structured plan that tells you what to do each time you go to the gym. Here's how to read one.

## Structure

Programs are organized like this:

```
Program (e.g., "Beginner Full Body")
  Week 1
    Day 1: Full Body A
    Day 2: Full Body B
    Day 3: Full Body A
  Week 2
    Day 1: Full Body A (slightly harder)
    Day 2: Full Body B (slightly harder)
    Day 3: Full Body A (slightly harder)
  ...
```

Each **week** contains a set of **workouts** (training days). Each workout contains a list of **exercises** with prescribed sets and reps.

## Progressive overload

The most important concept in strength training: **do a little more over time.**

This can mean:
- Adding 5 lbs to the bar
- Doing 1 more rep with the same weight
- Doing 1 more set
- Using better form (more range of motion, more control)

You don't need to increase every single session. But over weeks and months, the trend should be upward.

## Following a program vs. making it up

Beginners get much better results from following a program than from walking into the gym and picking random exercises. A good program:

- Balances muscle groups (so you don't skip legs)
- Progresses at the right pace
- Gives you a clear plan so you don't waste time deciding

## What if I miss a day?

Just pick up where you left off. If you missed Day 2, do Day 2 next time you're in the gym. Don't skip ahead, and don't try to double up.

## How long should I follow one program?

Stick with a program for at least 8-12 weeks before switching. Jumping between programs every few weeks doesn't give your body time to adapt and grow.
