# When to Increase Weight

Adding weight too fast leads to bad form and injury. Adding it too slowly means slower progress. Here's how to find the sweet spot.

## The simple rule

**Increase the weight when you can complete all prescribed sets and reps at RPE 7 or below for two sessions in a row.**

In plain English: if the weight feels pretty easy for two workouts in a row, it's time to go up.

## How much to increase

- **Machines:** Go up one notch on the pin (usually 10-15 lbs)
- **Dumbbells:** Go up 5 lbs (grab the next pair)
- **Barbell:** Add 5 lbs total (2.5 lb plate on each side)

Smaller jumps are better. If the gym has micro plates (1.25 lb), use them. Big jumps often feel too hard and force you to drop back down.

## What if the new weight is too hard?

If you increase the weight and can only do 7 reps instead of 10, that's fine. Stick with the new weight and try to add one more rep each session:

- Session 1: 10, 10, 10 at 50 lbs (easy -- time to go up)
- Session 2: 8, 7, 7 at 55 lbs (harder but manageable)
- Session 3: 9, 8, 8 at 55 lbs (getting better)
- Session 4: 10, 10, 9 at 55 lbs (almost there)
- Session 5: 10, 10, 10 at 55 lbs (nailed it)

This is progressive overload in action.

## Upper body vs. lower body

Lower body exercises (squats, leg press) can usually handle bigger weight jumps than upper body exercises (curls, shoulder press). Your legs are stronger -- that's normal.

## Patience

Progress isn't always linear. Some weeks you'll feel strong, some weeks you won't. As long as the trend over months is upward, you're doing great. Don't get discouraged by one bad session.
