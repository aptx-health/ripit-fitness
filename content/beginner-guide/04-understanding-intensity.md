# Understanding Intensity

How hard should each set feel? There are two simple scales.

## RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)

RPE is a 1-10 scale of how hard a set felt:

| RPE | What it feels like |
|-----|-------------------|
| **6** | Warm-up. You could do many more reps. |
| **7** | Moderate. You could do about 3 more reps. |
| **8** | Challenging. You could do about 2 more reps. |
| **9** | Very hard. You could maybe do 1 more rep. |
| **10** | Maximum effort. You could not do another rep. |

**For beginners, aim for RPE 7-8.** That means finishing each set feeling like you had 2-3 reps left in the tank. You should feel challenged but not destroyed.

## RIR (Reps in Reserve)

RIR is the flip side of RPE. It's how many more reps you think you could have done:

- **3 RIR** = You stopped with 3 reps left (same as RPE 7)
- **2 RIR** = You stopped with 2 reps left (same as RPE 8)
- **1 RIR** = You stopped with 1 rep left (same as RPE 9)
- **0 RIR** = You couldn't do another rep (same as RPE 10)

Some programs use RPE, some use RIR. They're the same idea from different directions.

## What does this mean in practice?

When your program says "3 sets of 10 at RPE 7," it means:

1. Pick a weight where you can do 10 reps
2. After rep 10, you should feel like you could have done about 3 more
3. If you could only do 10 and no more, the weight is too heavy
4. If you could easily do 15, the weight is too light

It takes a few sessions to calibrate this. That's normal. Start lighter and adjust.
