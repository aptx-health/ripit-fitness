# Article Review: Beginner Content (dev-articles.ts)

## General Feedback

The articles are strong overall. The tone is warm without being condescending, and the information is practical and well-scoped for someone who has never set foot in a gym. The writing mostly avoids AI slop — it reads like a knowledgeable friend explaining things, which is exactly right for the target audience (complete beginners, gym anxiety, Iron Works Boulder partnership).

A few patterns show up across multiple articles that are worth addressing:

**1. Negative parallelism creep.** Several articles use the "It's not X — it's Y" or "not X, just Y" reframe. Any single instance is fine, but across 13 articles they stack up and start to feel formulaic. I've flagged the specific ones below.

**2. Bold-first bullet lists.** Most bullet lists use `**Bold label:** description` formatting. This is one of the most recognizable AI formatting tells. Some of these are genuinely useful (like the RIR scale), but many would read better as plain bullets or short sentences.

**3. Repetitive section endings.** Several articles end with a tidy summary sentence that restates the thesis. "Your First Week," "Choosing the Right Weight," and "Progressive Overload" all do this. The articles are short enough that the reader doesn't need a recap — they just read the thing 60 seconds ago. Trim the endings and let the last substantive point be the last thing they read.

**4. Em dash frequency is fine** — generally 2-4 per article, within range. No issues there.

**5. The tone is genuinely good.** Phrases like "You're invisible in the best possible way" and "That took more effort than it takes the regular who's been going for years on autopilot" land well. Don't over-correct the tone in edits.

## Collection 1: Getting Started

### Your First Week: What to Expect

The strongest article in the set. Addresses the exact emotional arc a nervous beginner goes through. The DOMS explanation is perfectly pitched — gives the name, normalizes it, and moves on without over-explaining.

- **Line 64** — "Show up, follow the program, don't go too heavy. That's it. Everything else — optimal weight selection, understanding intensity, tracking progression — comes later. Right now your only job is to build the habit of walking through the door." This is a solid closer but the last sentence edges into motivational-poster territory. Consider cutting "Right now your only job is to build the habit of walking through the door" — the preceding sentence already says everything.
- **Line 58** — "This is the part that hooks people. Not a dramatic transformation, just the quiet realization that your body adapts fast when you give it a reason to." Two flags: "quiet realization" is an AI-ism ("quietly" family), and "Not X, just Y" is negative parallelism. Rephrase — something like: "This is the part that hooks people. You notice your body adapts fast when you give it a reason to."

### How to Read Your Program

Clean, practical, no fluff. Does exactly what the title promises.

- **Bold-first bullets** on lines 84-86 (`**3**`, `**12**`, `**RIR 3**`). This one actually works because it's explaining notation. Keep it.
- **Line 105** — "But that only works if you log what actually happened, not what the program told you to do." Good line. No notes.
- The numbered list (lines 95-99) is the right format here. Clear, sequential, actionable.

### Choosing the Right Weight

Good framework. The "start obviously too light and work up" advice is exactly right for someone anxious about looking stupid.

- **Line 116** — "The most common mistake beginners make is going too heavy too soon. The second most common mistake is worrying too much about picking the 'right' weight." This is close to "Not X. Not Y. Just Z." territory but it works because it's making two distinct points. Fine as-is.
- **Bold-first bullets** on lines 122-126 (RIR scale). This is reference material that will be re-read. Bold labels are justified here.
- **Line 132** — "When you see someone swinging their whole body to curl a dumbbell, that's ego lifting. The muscle they're trying to train isn't doing most of the work." Good concrete image. Relatable.
- **Lines 153-155** — The three bullets at the end ("You finished all sets..." / "You couldn't finish..." / "The weight felt right but...") use a soft bold-first pattern with the full scenario in bold. These would read better as short paragraphs or plain bullets since the scenarios are full sentences, not labels.

### Warming Up: Why and How

Tight and practical. The three-step structure is clear.

- **Line 166** — "Five to seven minutes before you start your first exercise." Nice — sets a concrete expectation. Beginners want to know "how long."
- **Bold-first bullets** on lines 170-174 (cardio options). These are just a plain list of options — no reason for bold. Remove the bold and make them simple dashes.
- **Lines 181-186** — Same issue. Plain list of movements, bold not needed.
- **Line 200** — "Before training, dynamic movement — the arm circles, leg swings, and torso twists above — does a better job of preparing your muscles." Clean. Answers the question they're definitely thinking.

### Staying Safe in the Gym

Important article. The pain vs. discomfort distinction is the single most valuable thing a beginner can learn.

- **Lines 217-221** — Bold-first pattern again for pain categories. This one is justified — these are genuinely distinct categories that need visual separation. Keep it.
- **Lines 227-229** — Bold-first bullets for machine safety ("Check the pin," "Adjust the seat," "Watch the cable path"). These work as scannable safety checks. Keep.
- **Line 242** — "Exhale during the hard part (pushing or pulling), inhale during the easy part (lowering)." Simple, actionable breathing cue. Good.
- No structural issues. This is one of the cleaner articles.

### Gym Etiquette: The Short Version

Good scope — covers what matters without being preachy.

- **Lines 258-262** — Bold-first bullets again. "Wipe down equipment" / "Rerack your weights" / "Don't sit on a machine scrolling." These are rules, and the bold formatting works as emphasis for the three things they need to remember. Keep.
- **Lines 272-276** — "What nobody will judge you for" section. The list items start with "-ing" verbs which is natural and reads well. No bold-first issue here.
- **Line 280** — "If someone has headphones in, they probably don't want to chat." Good social cue that addresses the "am I supposed to talk to people?" anxiety.

## Collection 2: Building Your Foundation

### Getting Comfortable with Machines

This is a strong reference article. Concrete, specific to the equipment they'll actually see.

- **Lines 306-309** — Bold-first bullets for machine-specific seat rules ("Chest press and shoulder press," "Lat pulldown," etc.). This is reference material meant for quick scanning mid-workout. Bold labels are warranted.
- **Line 298** — "Most commercial gyms use Matrix, Life Fitness, or similar brands. The adjustment mechanisms are nearly identical across all of them." Good — reduces anxiety about brand-specific confusion.
- **Line 332** — "And if the placard doesn't help — ask someone. Gym staff exist for exactly this question." Good closer. Direct.

### Dumbbell Basics

Practical and well-scoped. Covers the things a beginner wouldn't think to ask about.

- **Lines 365-368** — Bold-first bullets for grip types ("Neutral grip," "Supinated grip," "Pronated grip"). These are terms they'll see in their program. Bold labels help with scanning. Keep.
- **Line 349** — "Bend at your hips and knees (like a squat), grab the dumbbells, and stand up. Don't round your back and yank them off the rack." Specific and useful. The injury prevention advice is embedded naturally rather than being a scare tactic.
- **Line 373** — "A 20 lb dumbbell press feels heavier than 20 lbs on a chest press machine." Important calibration info. Prevents frustration when switching from machines to dumbbells.

### Rest Between Sets

Good topic for Collection 2. Beginners either rush or scroll too long.

- **Lines 392-396** — Bold-first bullets for rest guidelines ("Compound exercises," "Isolation exercises," "Warm-up sets"). Reference material with time ranges — bold is fine here.
- **Line 414** — "If the program says 3 sets and you're failing on set 2, try resting longer before you drop the weight. The rest might be the issue, not the weight." This is great practical advice that reframes the problem. The "not the weight" at the end is a mild negative parallelism but it's natural and serves the point. Leave it.
- The article is slightly repetitive between the "How long to rest" section and the "common beginner mistake" section — both say beginners don't rest enough. Consider tightening the common mistake section to just the actionable advice (rest longer before dropping weight) since the framework section already established the "how long" guidelines.

### Understanding RIR and Intensity

Good follow-up to the brief RIR intro in "Choosing the Right Weight."

- **Line 425** — "You've been using RIR for a couple of weeks now. You have a feel for it." Good — acknowledges where they are in their journey. Appropriate for Collection 2 timing.
- **Lines 448-450** — Bold-first bullets for RIR cues ("Speed," "Effort," "The last 2 reps test"). These are practical cues. Bold is fine.
- **Lines 457-463** — RPE/RIR mapping table. Clean and useful as reference. No issues.
- **Line 463** — "This app defaults to RIR because most people find 'how many more could I do?' easier to answer than 'what number out of 10 was that?'" Smart framing — answers the "why RIR and not RPE" question before they Google it.
- **Line 452** — "Don't stress about getting it exactly right. Being within 1 rep of your target is good enough. The important thing is that you're training with intentional effort, not just moving weight for the sake of counting reps." Mild negative parallelism at the end ("not just moving weight..."). It's natural here and only one instance in the article. Fine.

### Progressive Overload: Adding Weight Over Time

Solid. Gives a framework for when and how to progress, which is exactly what someone in week 2-4 needs.

- **Lines 497-500** — Bold-first bullets for stall strategies ("Stay and repeat," "Add reps first," "Drop and build back"). These are actionable options. Bold labels help distinguish the three approaches. Keep.
- **Lines 486-488** — Bold-first bullets for weight increments ("Machines," "Dumbbells," "Cables"). Reference material. Keep.
- **Lines 506-508** — "This is beginner gains — your nervous system is learning how to recruit muscle fibers efficiently, and the improvements come fast. After 6-12 months, progress slows down." Good expectation-setting. Avoids the fitness influencer trap of promising forever-gains.
- **Line 508** — "The principles stay the same — the timeline stretches." This is a tidy summary closer. Not terrible, but it's the kind of line that wraps a bow on things. Consider cutting it — the preceding sentences about experienced lifters fighting for a single rep improvement is a stronger, more concrete ending.

## Collection 3: Making It Stick

### Making the Gym Easy to Show Up To

Good behavioral design content. Reads like practical Atomic Habits advice without name-dropping the book.

- **Line 528** — "The biggest predictor of whether you'll work out on a given day isn't how motivated you feel — it's how many decisions stand between you and starting." This is negative parallelism ("isn't X — it's Y"). It's also the thesis of the article and it's well-stated. One instance is fine. But heads up — the article uses this structure again on line 559: "The fewer decisions you need to make, the more likely you are to go." That's the same point restated. This is one-point dilution — the article's thesis (reduce friction/decisions) is solid but gets restated across nearly every section. Consider whether sections "Make the default action 'go'" and "Remove the decision fatigue" are saying meaningfully different things. They could probably merge.
- **Lines 536-539** — Plain bullet list (no bold-first). Good.
- **Line 547** — "On low-motivation days, make a deal with yourself: just go and do the warm-up." This is great, concrete, actionable advice.
- **Line 557** — "Your program already tells you what to do each day. You don't have to plan a workout, choose exercises, or figure out sets and reps. Just open the app and follow along. That's an enormous amount of friction that's already been removed for you." This is product marketing embedded in an article. It's not wrong, but it crosses from education into selling. Tone it down — maybe just: "Your program handles the planning. You just show up and follow along."

### You're a Gym Person Now

The identity-shift article. This is the emotional capstone of the collection.

- **Line 573** — "It's not. They just have more reps in." Negative parallelism. In this case, the "It's not" is short and punchy in a way that could read as manufactured emphasis (short punchy fragment pattern). Consider integrating: "They're not. They just have more reps in." (changing "It's not" to "They're not" makes it flow from the preceding sentence more naturally).
- **Lines 576-578** — "There's a difference between 'I'm trying to get in shape' and 'I'm someone who goes to the gym.' The first framing treats gym-going as a temporary project with an endpoint. The second treats it as something you do, like brushing your teeth or making coffee in the morning." This is well done. The framing comparison is concrete and earns its length.
- **Line 590** — "A missed day doesn't feel like proof that 'this isn't for me.' It feels like a scheduling problem to solve." Good reframe. Practical.
- **Line 591** — "You don't quit brushing your teeth because you forgot one night." Strong analogy. Lands because it's simple and true.
- **Lines 596-597** — "You walked into a place that felt unfamiliar. You figured out the machines. You pushed through soreness and awkwardness and the voice that said you'd look silly. You came back and did it again." This is anaphora (repeated "You..." openings) but it works here because it's the emotional conclusion of the entire content library. Four repetitions, not ten. The emotional payoff justifies the structure. Keep it.
- **Line 598** — "Own it." Short punchy fragment as the final line. Normally I'd flag this, but as the last two words of the entire 13-article series, it works. It's earned.
